ADA LOVELACE

Ada Lovelace (1815-1852) was a visionary mathematician and writer, often regarded as the world's first computer programmer. Born Augusta Ada Byron, she made significant contributions to the field of computing during the early 19th century.

Life & Success Timeline

December 10, 1815

Lovelace is born as Augusta Ada Byron in London, England. Her father is the famous poet Lord Byron, and her mother is Lady Anne Isabella Milbanke Byron.

1828

Ada's mother, Lady Byron, hires tutors to educate her in mathematics and logic, hoping to steer her away from the poetic tendencies of her father.

1833

At the age of 17, Ada Lovelace meets Charles Babbage, a mathematician, inventor, and engineer known for his work on mechanical computers.

1834

Ada Lovelace marries William King, who later becomes the Earl of Lovelace. She becomes known as Ada Lovelace or Augusta Ada Lovelace.

1835

Ada Lovelace begins studying mathematics at the University of London, although she is not allowed to receive a degree due to her gender.

1838

Lovelace meets Mary Somerville, a renowned mathematician and scientist, who becomes her mentor and encourages her intellectual pursuits.

1842

Ada Lovelace translates an article on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine written by Italian engineer Luigi Menabrea. She adds extensive notes to the translation, including an algorithm that can be processed by the Analytical Engine. These notes, known as "Note G," are considered the first computer program ever written.

1843

Ada Lovelace's notes on the Analytical Engine, including the algorithm for calculating Bernoulli numbers, are published in an English science journal. These notes gain recognition as a significant contribution to computing.

1844

Ada Lovelace's last known mathematical work, "Note A," is published. It discusses the potential of the Analytical Engine to generate music, produce graphics, and explore mathematical concepts beyond simple calculations.

1852

Ada Lovelace dies at the age of 36 on November 27, 1852, due to uterine cancer. Her contributions to computing are largely forgotten for many years.

1940s-1950s

Ada Lovelace's work gains recognition as early computers are developed. Her notes on the Analytical Engine are rediscovered and acknowledged as significant contributions to computer science.

1980

The U.S. Department of Defense develops a high-level programming language and names it "Ada" in honor of Ada Lovelace. The language becomes widely used in military and aerospace applications.

Present day

Ada Lovelace is celebrated as a pioneer of computer science and a symbol of women's contributions to the field. Her legacy inspires and encourages women pursuing careers in STEM (science, technology, engineering, and mathematics) fields.
